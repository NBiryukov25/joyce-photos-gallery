The Officer-CH 1

Chapter 1 — The Appointment

May 2026

The morning light filtered through thin curtains, casting pale stripes across the unfamiliar bedroom. Joyce stirred awake, her body still adjusting to the new timezone, the new climate, the new everything. She was in America now, in a modest suburban house in Virginia that her husband David had rented for their fresh start. The K-1 visa application hung in bureaucratic limbo—she was legal, technically, pending approval, but that pending status felt like a thread that could snap at any moment.

David was already awake beside her, his hand tracing lazy patterns across her hip. They had been married for six months, a whirlwind romance that started when he was working overseas and ended with her packing two suitcases and leaving everything familiar behind. The adjustment had been overwhelming—the language differences despite her fluency, the cultural shifts, the isolation of being thousands of miles from her family and friends.

"Morning," David murmured, his voice rough with sleep. He pulled her closer, his body warm against hers, and Joyce felt the familiar flutter of desire mixed with the comfort of security. In these moments, wrapped in his arms in their bed, she could forget about the paperwork, the interviews, the constant low-grade anxiety of proving her right to exist in this country.

They made love slowly, unhurried, the morning light growing stronger around them. David moved above her, his weight familiar and welcome, his breath hot against her neck. Joyce arched into him, her fingers digging into his shoulders, losing herself in the physical connection that transcended language and borders. When he climaxed inside her, he groaned her name against her skin, and she held him tightly, wishing they could stay like this forever—locked away from the world and its demands.

Afterward, they lay tangled in the sheets, the ceiling fan spinning lazily overhead. David's hand rested on her stomach, possessive and tender. Joyce traced the line of his jaw, feeling the stubble there, memorizing the planes of his face. She had given up so much to be here with him, but in moments like this, it felt worth it.

"Today's the appointment," she said quietly, the words hanging in the humid air.

David sighed, his expression shifting from contentment to frustration. "I know. I hate that I can't go with you."

"You have the meeting with the client. The Henderson account."

"It's just bad timing." He sat up, running a hand through his hair. "Are you sure you don't want to reschedule? We could push it back a week, go together..."

Joyce shook her head, sitting up and pulling the sheet around her chest. "We can't reschedule. The notice said if I miss this appointment, they could deny the petition. I can't risk it, David. I can't risk us."

He turned to her, his eyes serious. "I know how much this means to you. How much you've sacrificed." He cupped her face in his hands. "You're going to do great. Just answer their questions honestly. You've done nothing wrong. We fell in love, we got married, we're building a life. That's all they need to know."

Joyce nodded, but the anxiety remained coiled in her stomach. She had heard stories—online forums, whispered warnings from other immigrants—about immigration officers who looked for any excuse to deny applications, who treated fiancée visas like scams to be uncovered rather than legitimate relationships to be verified. The power imbalance was terrifying; one wrong answer, one misplaced document, and she could find herself on a plane back home, her marriage effectively annulled by bureaucracy.

They showered separately, the water pressure different from what she was used to, another small reminder that she was a stranger here. Joyce dressed carefully, choosing a modest navy dress that she hoped projected respectability and stability. She pulled her dark hair back in a severe bun, wanting to look serious, responsible, worthy of acceptance.

In the kitchen, David poured her coffee and set out toast she wouldn't eat. Her nerves had killed her appetite. Carla—her daughter from her first marriage, eight years old and still adjusting to having a new stepfather—shuffled in wearing pajamas, clutching her stuffed rabbit.

"Do I have to go?" Carla asked, her voice small.

"Yes, baby. They need to see you too. You're part of my application." Joyce knelt down, straightening Carla's hair. "It'll be quick, I promise. Then we'll get ice cream after."

David kissed them both goodbye at the door, pressing a twenty-dollar bill into Joyce's hand. "For the ice cream. And Joyce—call me the second you're done. I don't care if I'm in the meeting. Step out and call me."

The drive to the USCIS field office took forty minutes through traffic that made Joyce's hands sweat on the steering wheel. She wasn't used to American highways, the speed and aggression of the other drivers. Carla sat in the back seat, quietly reading a book, blessedly unaware of the stakes of this appointment.

The building was a featureless concrete structure indistinguishable from the other government offices surrounding it. Joyce parked in the visitor lot and sat for a moment, gathering her courage. She checked her folder for the third time—marriage certificate, photos, bank statements, David's employment letter, her medical records, the endless forms filled out in triplicate. Everything was there. Everything had to be there.

"Mom?" Carla's voice cut through her thoughts. "Are we going in?"

Joyce forced a smile. "Yes, baby. We're going in."

The interior was exactly as sterile and intimidating as she had imagined. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. A security guard checked their IDs and directed them to a waiting room filled with plastic chairs and anxious people from a dozen different countries. Joyce clutched her folder to her chest and tried to slow her racing heart.

When the door opened and a voice called her name, she stood on unsteady legs. The officer who emerged was a heavyset man with a hard face and eyes that assessed her with cold calculation. He didn't smile as he checked his clipboard.

"Joyce? And this is Carla?" He barely glanced at her daughter. "Follow me."

They were led down a narrow hallway to a small interrogation room. The officer—his badge read Miller—gestured to the chairs and closed the door with a deliberate click that echoed in the small space. Joyce sat down, her dress sticking to the backs of her thighs, her mouth dry.

Miller leaned back in his chair, the leather squeaking under his weight, and let his eyes drag slowly over Joyce's trembling hands before settling on Carla. The girl shifted in the hard plastic chair, her sneakers scuffing the linoleum floor.

The air in the small room felt heavy, recycled and stale, carrying the faint scent of floor wax that made Joyce's stomach turn. She kept her head bowed, staring at the scratches on the metal table, while beside her, Carla shifted in the hard plastic chair, her sneakers scuffing the linoleum floor.

"Your paperwork is a mess," he said, his voice low and smooth, lacking any real urgency but filled with a quiet menace. Miller tapped a thick stack of papers with his knuckles, a deliberate, rhythmic sound that echoed like a countdown. He didn't look at the forms; he kept his gaze fixed on the mother and daughter, enjoying the way they flinched at every noise from the hallway. "Seems you missed a deadline or two. Could be a problem for your status."

Joyce finally looked up, her mouth dry. She tried to speak, but her voice cracked, so she just swallowed hard and nodded, clutching her purse tighter. Miller smiled then, a thin expression that didn't reach his eyes, and pulled the file closer, blocking his view of them as if they were nothing more than another case number to be processed, or toyed with.

"But I can fix this," Miller said, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial hush that barely carried across the desk. He picked up a pen, clicking it rhythmically, the sound sharp and jarring in the quiet room. "I can make the errors disappear. No one needs to know about the discrepancies. Your husband certainly doesn't need to know how close you came to losing everything."

Joyce's hands gripped the edge of her purse until her knuckles turned white. She glanced at Carla, who was staring at her shoes, trying to make herself small and invisible. The fluorescent light overhead hummed, a constant, headache-inducing buzz that made the silence between them feel thick and suffocating. Miller watched Joyce closely, his eyes narrowing as he measured her desperation, savoring the power he held over their future.

"Meet me in the parking lot after five," Miller continued, leaning forward slightly, invading her personal space. "I'll have the corrected forms in my car. We can sign them there, off the record." He paused, letting the implication hang in the stale air. "And Joyce? Don't tell your husband. He wouldn't understand the special arrangement we're making. He might think you did something wrong to jeopardize the visa."

He stood up abruptly, signaling the end of the meeting, and buttoned his uniform jacket. The badge on his chest glinted under the harsh lights. Joyce felt a cold knot tighten in her stomach, but she nodded slowly, trapped by the threat of deportation and the man's unspoken demands. Miller walked to the door and held it open, waiting for them to leave, his shadow stretching long across the floor.
