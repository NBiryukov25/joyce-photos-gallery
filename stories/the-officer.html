The Officer

Chapter 1 — The Appointment

May 2026

Officer Miller leaned back in his chair, the leather squeaking under his weight, and let his eyes drag slowly over Joyce's trembling hands before settling on Kathleen. The air in the small room felt heavy, recycled and stale, carrying the faint scent of floor wax that made Joyce's stomach turn. She kept her head bowed, staring at the scratches on the metal table, while beside her, Kathleen shifted in the hard plastic chair, her sneakers scuffing the linoleum floor.

Miller tapped a thick stack of papers with his knuckles, a deliberate, rhythmic sound that echoed like a countdown. "Your paperwork is a mess," he said, his voice low and smooth, lacking any real urgency but filled with a quiet menace. He didn't look at the forms; he kept his gaze fixed on the mother and daughter, enjoying the way they flinched at every noise from the hallway. "Seems you missed a deadline or two. Could be a problem for your status."

Joyce finally looked up, her mouth dry. She tried to speak, but her voice cracked, so she just swallowed hard and nodded, clutching her purse tighter. Miller smiled then, a thin expression that didn't reach his eyes, and pulled the file closer, blocking his view of them as if they were nothing more than another case number to be processed, or toyed with.

"But I can fix this," Miller said, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial hush that barely carried across the desk. He picked up a pen, clicking it rhythmically, the sound sharp and jarring in the quiet room. "I can make the errors disappear. No one needs to know about the discrepancies. Your husband certainly doesn't need to know how close you came to losing everything."

Joyce's hands gripped the edge of her purse until her knuckles turned white. She glanced at Kathleen, who was staring at her shoes, trying to make herself small and invisible. The fluorescent light overhead hummed, a constant, headache-inducing buzz that made the silence between them feel thick and suffocating. Miller watched Joyce closely, his eyes narrowing as he measured her desperation, savoring the power he held over their future.

"Meet me in the parking lot after five," Miller continued, leaning forward slightly, invading her personal space. "I'll have the corrected forms in my car. We can sign them there, off the record." He paused, letting the implication hang in the stale air. "And Joyce? Don't tell your husband. He wouldn't understand the special arrangement we're making. He might think you did something wrong to jeopardize the visa."

He stood up abruptly, signaling the end of the meeting, and buttoned his uniform jacket. The badge on his chest glinted under the harsh lights. Joyce felt a cold knot tighten in her stomach, but she nodded slowly, trapped by the threat of deportation and the man's unspoken demands. Miller walked to the door and held it open, waiting for them to leave, his shadow stretching long across the floor.

The late afternoon sun beat down on the asphalt, turning the parking lot into a furnace. Joyce stood near the edge of the lot, shielding her eyes with one hand while keeping a firm grip on Kathleen's shoulder with the other. Cars were leaving now, federal employees heading home for the day, their tires crunching loudly over the loose gravel. Kathleen tugged at her mother's sleeve, her face pale and slick with sweat, looking up with eyes full of questions Joyce wasn't ready to answer.

A black sedan rolled out of the reserved row and idled near the exit, the tinted windows hiding the interior. Miller didn't wave or call out; he just waited, the engine rumbling low and steady. Joyce felt a bead of sweat slide down her spine, chilling her despite the heat. She squeezed Kathleen's hand, her thumb rubbing over the knuckles, trying to offer comfort she didn't feel herself. The air smelled of exhaust and hot rubber, heavy and suffocating.

"Go wait by the bus stop," Joyce said, her voice sounding thin and brittle in the open air. She pointed toward the bench under the sparse shade of a dying oak tree. "I have to get the papers. It won't take long."

Kathleen hesitated, her feet planted on the pavement, looking from the car back to her mother. "I want to stay with you."

"No," Joyce said firmly, pushing her daughter gently toward the sidewalk. "Please, Kathleen. Just go. I'll be right there."

Miller tapped the horn, a short, sharp sound that cut through the evening noise. Joyce watched Kathleen walk away, her shoulders slumped, before she forced herself to move toward the idling car. The passenger window rolled down slowly with an electric whir, revealing Miller’s smirk and the cool, conditioned air blowing from inside the dashboard.

“You made me wait,” Miller said, his voice smooth and dismissive as he drummed his fingers on the steering wheel. He didn’t look at her face, his eyes crawling over her blouse instead. “Get in. We can’t discuss your file status out here where anyone might hear.”

Joyce’s hand froze on the door handle, the metal burning hot against her palm. She glanced back toward the bus stop. Kathleen was sitting on the bench, swinging her legs, too far away to hear but close enough to watch. The distance felt like a chasm. The air conditioning from the car vent washed over her, carrying the scent of peppermint and stale tobacco, a sharp contrast to the dusty heat outside.

“I don’t want to get in,” Joyce said, her voice barely rising above the idling engine. She tightened her grip on the door frame, her knuckles turning white. “Just give me the forms. Please.”

Miller laughed, a dry, humorless sound that grated in the small space. He shifted the car into park but kept his foot heavy on the brake. “Forms? You think this is about paperwork?” He turned finally, his eyes cold and hard, pinning her in place. “You’re illegal, Joyce. Or you will be by morning if I say so. You want to stay? You want your kid to stay? Then you do what you’re told.”

He reached over and unlocked the door with a deliberate click, the sound echoing like a gunshot in the quiet lot. “Don’t make me come out there.”

Joyce swallowed hard, the taste of copper filling her mouth. She looked at Kathleen one last time, then pulled the door open and slid onto the leather seat. The lock clicked down automatically, sealing them in.

“Are you gonna make a ten year-old kid wait out there alone?” Miller asked, his voice dropping to a low, mocking whisper. He nodded toward the bus stop, where Kathleen sat swinging her legs, oblivious to the predator watching her. “It’s getting dark. Lots of bad people around here. You wouldn’t want something to happen to her just because her mother was difficult.”

Joyce’s stomach turned over, a sick lurch that nearly brought up the coffee she’d forced down earlier. She stared straight ahead at the dusty dashboard, her hands clenched in her lap so tightly her nails dug into her skin. The air in the car felt heavy, suffocating, like the air before a storm. She knew he wasn’t talking about criminals on the street. He was talking about himself. He held all the power here, a badge and a gun and the ability to snap their lives in half with a stamp.

“No,” Joyce whispered, the word scraping her throat. She couldn’t look at him. If she looked, she was afraid she might scream, or worse, break down completely. “I’m listening.”

Miller chuckled, a dry, satisfied sound that made the hair on her arms stand up. He shifted in his seat, turning his body toward her, his knee brushing against hers in the cramped space. “Good. That’s a good start. Now, let’s talk about what you’re willing to do to keep that little girl safe.” He reached into his breast pocket and pulled out a folded envelope, tapping it against his palm. “Because this right here? It’s the only thing standing between her and a one-way ticket back to the hell you came from.”

“So, what’s it gonna be?” Miller asked, leaning closer until Joyce could smell the stale coffee on his breath. He let the envelope drop onto the center console, right between her trembling hands. The paper looked thin, ordinary, but it felt like a loaded gun sitting there. Sweat trickled down Joyce’s spine, soaking into the fabric of her shirt. She looked out the window at the bus stop across the street. A few other people were waiting now, but Kathleen was still alone on the bench, kicking a stone into the gutter. She looked so small against the gathering dark.

Miller reached out and turned the key in the ignition. The engine roared to life, vibrating through the seat and into Joyce’s bones. The air conditioning blasted on, freezing cold against her damp skin. “We can go for a little drive,” he said, his eyes locked on her face, watching for the crack. “Somewhere private. We can discuss your options. Or I can write ‘Denied’ on that file right now and call a transport van for your kid. Your choice.” He shifted into gear, his hand resting casually on the stick shift, his fingers inches from her thigh.

Joyce’s heart hammered against her ribs like a trapped bird. She couldn’t let them take Kathleen. She couldn’t go back. The thought of her daughter alone in a detention center, or worse, sent a jolt of terrified adrenaline through her veins. She slowly reached out and placed her hand over the envelope, her fingers shaking so badly she nearly knocked it onto the floor mat. “Don’t hurt her,” she said, her voice barely audible over the engine. “Please.”

Miller smiled, a slow, ugly stretch of his lips that didn’t reach his cold eyes. He put the car in drive and eased his foot off the brake. “Just keep your head down and do exactly what I say,” he muttered, pulling out of the parking spot and turning away from the bus stop. The last thing Joyce saw was the yellow blink of the bus approaching, too far away to help her now.

Miller drove in silence for several minutes, navigating the car through the industrial outskirts of the city until the buildings grew sparse and the streetlights flickered out. He steered the vehicle into a deserted lot behind a shuttered warehouse, killing the engine but leaving the headlights on to cut through the surrounding darkness. The sudden silence was heavy, broken only by the cooling ticks of the metal hood. He turned in his seat to face her, the harsh light casting deep shadows across his face, making his expression unreadable and cold.

“You think this is just about paperwork?” Miller asked, his voice low and grating. He reached over and rested his hand on her knee, his fingers digging into her skin through the fabric of her pants. “I know what you people are like. I know you’ll do anything to stay.” He slid his hand higher up her thigh, squeezing hard enough to make her flinch. “I want what your husband gets. I want the same loving he gets at home. And don’t pretend you don’t know what I mean.”

Joyce stiffened, pressing her back flat against the passenger door, trying to put as much distance between them as the small car allowed. She shook her head quickly, her breath coming in short, sharp gasps. “No, please, I can’t,” she stammered, her eyes darting to the locked door handle.

Miller leaned in closer, his face inches from hers, the smell of stale tobacco overwhelming her senses. “You can, and you will,” he whispered, his tone dropping to a dangerous hiss. “If you say one word I don’t like, or if you try to stop me, you won’t see that little girl at the bus stop for a long time. I’ll make sure she’s sent so far away you’ll never find her.” He paused, letting the threat hang in the air like a physical weight. “Now, show me how grateful you are.”

The air in the car grew stifling, thick with the scent of his cheap cologne and the metallic tang of fear. Joyce stared at the dashboard lights, her vision blurring as she tried to process the ultimatum. Her hands gripped the edge of the seat until her knuckles turned white, the only anchor she had in a world suddenly tilting on its axis. Miller didn’t wait for a verbal answer; he took her silence as submission and leaned back, unbuckling his belt with a deliberate, metallic clink that echoed in the small space like a gunshot.

“Don’t make me drag you out here,” he said, his voice dropping an octave, losing the pretense of official business. He pushed the seat back as far as it would go, the mechanism groaning in protest. The shadows stretched across his face, highlighting the cruel twist of his lips. He reached out, grabbing a fistful of her blouse, his eyes locked on hers with a predator’s focus. “You think this is a game? I can end you with a phone call. One signature and you’re trash on the street.”

Joyce’s breath hitched, a sob catching in her throat that she choked back down. She thought of Kathleen waiting at the kitchen table, probably worried, doing homework by the dim light of the stove. The image grounded her, cutting through the panic like a knife. She couldn’t fight him, not here, not with the power he held over their heads, but she couldn’t just dissolve either. Her hand moved slowly, trembling as she reached for the door handle, not to open it, but to steady herself against the cold plastic.

Miller laughed, a dry, rattling sound, and grabbed her wrist, pulling her toward him. “That’s better. You learn fast.” He yanked her closer, forcing her to lose her balance and tumble against the center console. The rough fabric of his uniform scratched against her cheek, and she squeezed her eyes shut, blocking out the sight of his triumph, trying to retreat to a place inside herself where he couldn’t reach her, even as his hands began to roam with rough, invasive urgency.

Miller’s fingers dug into her shoulder, steering her across the center console with the rough efficiency of handling cargo. The vinyl seat squeaked under the shifting weight, a grating noise that seemed deafening in the quiet room. Joyce squeezed her eyes shut so tight that spots of light danced in the darkness behind her lids, but she couldn't block out the heat of his breath or the smell of stale tobacco. “Look at me,” he commanded, his voice lacking any true human warmth, just the cold expectation of obedience.

She forced her eyes open, staring past him at the streetlight outside the window, fixing her gaze on the moths fluttering against the glass. Anything to disconnect her mind from what his hands were doing. He noticed the detachment instantly and grabbed her chin, jerking her face around until she had no choice but to look into his pale, watery eyes. “You think you're somewhere else? You're right here. You're nothing without this stamp,” he hissed, tapping her chest hard with his knuckles.

Pain bloomed sharply, grounding her back in the horrible reality of the front seat. She bit the inside of her cheek until she tasted iron, using the sting to keep from screaming. He laughed, a low, rumbling sound that vibrated against her, and pressed closer, forcing her back against the passenger door. The handle dug into her spine, a hard reminder of the trapped space. “Good girl. Now let's make sure you understand exactly what's required to stay in this country.”

The leather of his belt creaked as he shifted his weight, the sound sharp and final in the cramped space. He didn't ask; he just guided her head down with a heavy hand on the crown of her skull, steering her like he was correcting a wrong turn. Joyce’s stomach turned over, a cold knot tightening as the reality of his position sank in. The air in the car felt thick, suffocatingly warm, and she struggled to find a rhythm that would make this end faster. She focused on the fabric of his trousers, the rough texture against her palms, trying to disconnect her mind from the mechanical movements of her jaw.
