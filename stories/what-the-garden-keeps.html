Personal Essay

What the Garden Keeps

Thailand, July 2018

By Sheryl Joyce

I — Room 203

I had been in Bangkok for three days before I let myself believe he was actually coming.

The room at the guesthouse on Sukhumvit Soi 11 was small and smelled faintly of jasmine and air conditioning — the particular combination that I now associate with anticipation. I had arranged the small bottles of lotion on the bathroom shelf twice. I had read the same paragraph in my book six times without retaining a word.

A year. It had been almost exactly a year since we had last been in the same room together. We had managed it — the calls at strange hours, the photographs sent across time zones, the long messages that arrived like letters used to. But a year is a long time to love someone from a distance. The body forgets certain things. Or rather — it remembers them too well, which becomes its own kind of forgetting.

When the knock came, I did not rush to the door. I stood in the center of the room for a moment, aware that everything was about to reorganise itself. That whatever version of the year had been true up until that moment was about to be replaced by something that would feel more real, and that the waiting — which had been its own complete world — was ending.

I opened the door.

He was standing there with his bag still on his shoulder and the particular look he gets when he has been traveling for a long time and has not yet settled back into his face. He looked at me and didn't say anything for a moment.

"Hi," he said finally.

"Hi," I said.

It is strange how the simplest words are the ones that carry everything.

II — Suvarnabhumi, 9:10 AM

He had flown through the night — London to Dubai to Bangkok, the kind of journey that arrives on the other side of the world with time already slightly out of joint.

I had not gone to the airport. We had agreed on this: that it would be better to meet somewhere quieter, to have a room and a door and some privacy for the first hour. Airports make everything theatrical in a way that doesn't suit us. We are not people who run toward each other in arrivals halls. We are people who stand still and let the feeling come.

He sent me a message from the taxi: In the car. Bangkok is bright. Miss you and also slightly terrified of you.

I had laughed at that, alone in the room, and felt the tension in my shoulders release a little. This was still us. A year had not changed the way we talked to each other.

The city outside the window was already loud with the particular morning energy of Bangkok — the motorbikes and the vendors and the hotel shuttles, the heat already building even though it was barely past nine. I watched it from the window and thought: he is moving through all of this right now. He is getting closer.

III — The Road to Pattaya

We left Bangkok on the second day.

Not because we had seen enough of the city — we hadn't, really — but because we both felt the pull of somewhere quieter. Richard had found a place outside Pattaya, a smaller resort with a garden and a pool and the kind of unhurried mornings we had both been imagining for months.

The drive took just over two hours. We sat in the back of the hired car and watched the city give way to highway, to industrial outskirts, to the low green hills of the Eastern Seaboard. Richard slept for part of it — or half-slept, his head tipped against the window, his hand in mine. I stayed awake and looked at the landscape and felt the strangeness of being in motion with him after so long of being still and apart.

There is something about travel with someone you love that strips things back to essentials. You are reduced to just the two of you and whatever you have with you. The rest — the routines, the geography of your separate lives, the careful management of distance — falls away. For the length of the drive, we were simply two people going somewhere together.

I thought: I had forgotten this. The ease of it.

IV — The Garden at Nong Nooch

We went to Nong Nooch on the third afternoon.

I had not expected to be moved by a botanical garden. I had expected something manicured and touristic — and it was, in parts, the topiary animals and the shaped hedges and the organised pathways. But there were also sections where the planting was dense and old and the canopy closed over the paths and you could forget, for stretches, that you were anywhere designed at all.

We walked slowly. We had no schedule, no reason to hurry. The light came through the trees in the particular way that tropical light does — not the gentle diffusion of temperate afternoons, but something more deliberate, more vertical, broken into beams by the canopy above.

Richard kept stopping to look at things. The particular orange of a bird of paradise flower. The way a stone path curved toward a reflection pool. He has always been this way — attentive to detail, unwilling to let the beautiful thing pass by unremarked.

"This is what I wanted," he said at one point. Not to me exactly, not as a statement that required an answer. Just the acknowledgment of it: that this — the afternoon, the garden, the being here together — was what the year of waiting had been for.

I took his hand and we kept walking.

Later, we found a bench in a shaded corner and sat for a long time without talking. The garden moved around us — other visitors on the paths, birds in the upper canopy, the faint sound of water somewhere nearby. But in our particular corner it was quiet enough to hear each other breathing.

I thought about how little of what matters between people can be transmitted across distance. Not the warmth of a hand. Not the particular quality of someone's silence. Not the way a year can be settled in an afternoon, in a garden, without any words at all.

V — The Last Morning

He left on a Thursday.

We had five days in the end — two in Bangkok, three at the resort outside the city. It had been enough to feel real and not enough to feel complete, which is perhaps the best you can hope for when the logistics of a life together are still being worked out across time zones and immigration systems and the particular complexities of our respective situations.

The last morning was slow. We did not make plans or fill the time with activity. We had coffee on the small terrace of our room and watched the garden wake up — the gardeners arriving with their tools, the birds in the frangipanis, the light going from gold to white as the morning progressed.

He said: "I don't want to think about the flight."

I said: "Then don't."

So we didn't. We sat in the morning and didn't think about it, and when the car came to take him to the airport we hugged in the car park in the particular way you hug someone when you don't know the next date and the next date feels suddenly very far away.

"Soon," he said. Not a question. A promise, and also a request.

"Soon," I agreed.

VI — After

I stayed in Thailand for another week after he left.

I had arranged it deliberately — the buffer, the time alone in a place that was still new to me. I have learned that transitions need their own space. That going directly from being with him to being without him, with no interval, makes the absence too sharp.

I went back to Bangkok. I walked through neighborhoods I hadn't seen, ate at places I chose entirely for myself, sat in a coffee shop in Ari for three hours one afternoon and wrote in my notebook and talked to no one. It was good. It was the particular good of being alone after intimacy — the way solitude has a different texture when you have been recently held.

At night I looked at the photographs we had taken in Nong Nooch. The bench in the shaded corner. The bird of paradise flower. The reflection pool with the trees bending over it. Small archives of afternoons that had already become memory.

The garden, I thought, keeps what you bring to it. The weight of the waiting, the relief of the arrival, the specific quality of five days that will not come again in exactly that form — all of it absorbed into the afternoon light, held somewhere in the dense planting, in the shaded corners, in the sound of water nearby.

You carry some of it with you when you leave. The rest stays behind. That is how it works with the places that matter.

That is what the garden keeps.
