What the Garden Keeps

Thailand, July 2018

May 2026

I.  Room 203

She arrived before him, the way the faithful always do.

Bangkok received her the way it received everyone — without ceremony, without preference, with the same thick press of heat and jasmine and exhaust that it offered every arrival. She cleared customs alone, bought a SIM card at a kiosk beneath a ceiling fan making a lazy argument against the air, and took a taxi to a hotel whose photographs had been more generous than its reality.

Room 203. The windows were streaked. The wall adapter didn't match the one she'd brought from Hong Kong. There was no toothbrush in the bathroom.

She sat on the edge of the bed and felt the particular quality of waiting that is specific to people who have waited a long time for something that is finally genuinely about to arrive.

Nearly a year of mornings. Nearly a year of his voice arriving through a phone speaker, his face pixelating on a screen — the small digital world they had built between Portland, Maine and Causeway Bay, Hong Kong — two places so thoroughly unlike each other that the only thing they shared, apparently, was the two people inside them reaching across the distance every single day without fail.

She had his words memorized. She knew the rhythm of his sentences, the warmth that came through even in his typing. She knew how he laughed. She knew, in the way you know a piece of music you have listened to hundreds of times, every shade of his voice.

She did not know, yet, how he smelled. She did not know how his arms felt around her. She did not know what it was like to stand in the same room as him and hear his voice arrive through actual air rather than through a speaker.

She was about to find out.

She put her phone on the charger — the adapter she'd packed herself, fortunately — and lay on top of the covers in the Bangkok heat, and thought about the fact that he was somewhere over the Atlantic right now, then somewhere over the Gulf, aimed toward her like a letter finally in motion after a year of being composed.

She did not sleep. But she rested. And outside, Bangkok went about its enormous, indifferent, beautiful life.

II.  Suvarnabhumi AM

Richard's flight landed at 9 in the morning.

She knew his itinerary the way she knew his voice — by heart, with the investment of someone who had read it many times. Etihad flight 4, Boston to New York to Abu Dhabi to Bangkok. He had been traveling for close to twenty hours. She imagined him somewhere in that journey — in the Abu Dhabi terminal at midnight, under the vast white geometry of its ceiling, tired and awake in the way only long-haul travel makes you tired and awake — and she had sent him a message he probably wouldn't read until he landed:

Take care of your belongings. God bless you always. I love you.

She was not at the arrivals gate when the doors opened. She was in a coffee shop across the terminal, because the waiting in one place had become unbearable and moving had seemed like the next best thing to do. She had a coffee she wasn't drinking. She was watching the doors.

Then she saw him.

He was taller than she had understood from the screen. Broader. He was pulling a carry-on and wearing the slightly glazed expression of someone who had crossed many time zones on willpower alone, and he was scanning the terminal with the particular focused energy of a man looking for someone specific in a crowd of strangers.

And then he saw her.

She stood up from the table. The coffee cup was still in her hand and she put it down without looking at it. Later she would not remember crossing the space between them. She would only remember that there was distance and then there wasn't, and then his arms were around her and he was saying something into her hair that she couldn't quite hear over the airport noise — something low and warm — and she pressed her face against his shoulder and closed her eyes and thought: so this is what it's like. This is what it's like to have him be real.

He smelled like airplane and exhaustion and, underneath both of those things, something that was just him — specific and unmistakable, the way people always have a scent that no description prepares you for. She stayed where she was for a long moment. She did not want to move. She was aware, distantly, of the airport continuing around them — the announcements, the rolling luggage, the families and business people and tourists all going somewhere — and none of it mattered at all.

"Hi," he said, finally pulling back just enough to look at her.

She laughed. It came out wrong — too full of everything she was feeling, not quite a laugh, not quite a cry, something in between that she had no name for.

"Hi," she said back.

He was still holding her face in both his hands. His thumbs found her cheekbones and rested there gently, like someone making sure something precious was real. She looked up at him and thought about how many times she had seen this face through a screen — pixelating, freezing, recomposing itself through poor Wi-Fi signals at two in the morning — and how entirely different it was to have it here in the light, looking at her the way he was looking at her.

"I made it," he said.

"You made it," she agreed.

III.  The Road to Pattaya

They took a shared transfer south, along the Gulf of Thailand, the city peeling back into highway and then into roadside markets and temple gates and long flat stretches of palm.

He fell asleep against the window forty minutes in, his head tipped back, his breathing slow and deep, and she did not mind at all. She watched him sleep. She watched the country go past. She thought about how she had suggested Thailand — this specific neutral territory, neither her country nor his, a place where they could both be equally foreign, equally arrived — and how right it had turned out to be.

She had looked up this coastline months ago, the way you research a future you aren't sure will materialize. The Gulf waters. The wide beaches. The way the light came in off the sea in the late afternoon. She had stored all of it away, a private archive of a trip she wanted to take, knowing that wanting it was not the same as having it.

She had it now.

When he woke, somewhere on the outskirts of Pattaya, he reached across and took her hand. Just like that — no preamble, no announcement. He simply found her hand and held it, and she let him, and they sat that way for the rest of the drive while the Gulf glittered to their left through the palms.

IV.  The Garden at Nong Nooch

She had read about the garden in a travel guide. It was famous for its botanical collections — acres of cultivated tropical landscape, orchid houses, topiary shaped into animals and patterns, pathways winding between canopies of color. She had put it on the list quietly, hoping he'd agree, and when she mentioned it on their second day he had said yes immediately, the way he always said yes to things she suggested, with a genuine uncomplicated enthusiasm that she had grown to love about him.

They arrived in the early morning, before the heat made movement difficult. The garden was already open, the pathways mostly empty, the light coming through the overhead fronds in long diagonal bars of gold and green. Everything was extravagantly alive — the colors implausible, the flowers competing in a spectacle of reds and purples and yellows that seemed almost artificial in their perfection.

She took photographs of everything. She took photographs of him standing in front of a wall of bougainvillea, squinting into the sun, laughing at something she'd said. She took photographs of orchids. She took a photograph of their hands held together against the backdrop of an ornamental pond, the surface green and still, the lotus flowers rising out of it with that particular Thai patience.

He took photographs of her.

She knew, looking at his face when he held up the phone, that he was not just taking pictures the way tourists take pictures. He was documenting something. He was trying to hold onto the week by recording it carefully, because they both understood, without saying so, that it would end. That she would go back to Hong Kong and he would go back to Maine, and the screens would return, and the time zones would resume their work of pulling them apart.

They both knew this. It sat between them quietly — not as grief, but as the awareness that made each hour matter more.

He stopped on a path between two rows of towering palms and turned to her.

"I need to tell you something," he said.

She waited.

"I've been trying to figure out how to say it since I landed, and I can't find anything that isn't inadequate."

"Tell me anyway," she said.

He looked at her for a moment. The garden was quiet around them. Somewhere in the canopy, a bird was doing something complicated and joyful.

"I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be," he said. "For the first time in a long time, I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be."

She looked at him. She felt something shift in her chest — not break, not open exactly, something more like a settling. Like a thing that has been held at tension for a long time, finally allowed to rest.

She took his hand.

They walked.

V.  The Last Morning

On the last morning he was already awake when she opened her eyes. The light in the room was still the pale grey of early day, the curtains doing an imprecise job of keeping out the Gulf dawn. He was sitting on the edge of the bed, looking at his phone — but not in the way he looked at his phone when he was reading something. More the way people look at things they are not actually seeing.

"You're awake," she said.

"Couldn't sleep."

She reached over and touched the back of his shoulder. She could feel him make the small adjustment that meant he was aware of her hand there, leaning into it slightly — the language of bodies that had spent a week learning each other.

"Come downstairs with me," he said. "One more coffee."

The café at the base of the hotel was called the Lee Café — small and local and already awake, the staff moving around in the way of people who have been up since five. He ordered for them. He knew how she took her coffee by now — the way you know a thing after seven mornings of getting it right.

They sat across from each other at a table by the window, and the street outside was beginning its Pattaya business, and neither of them said much.

She looked at him.

She had been looking at him for a week through actual air, at a distance you could reach across. She had watched him sleep. She had watched him laugh — that particular laugh, better in person than through a speaker, bigger and more complete. She had watched him try the local food with the specific expression of a man from Maine encountering flavors he had no framework for. She had watched him look at her across tables, through the steam of meals, in the particular light of late afternoon on the Gulf of Thailand, with the focused tenderness of someone who is paying close attention because he knows the thing he is looking at matters.

"I don't want to go," he said.

"I know," she said.

"I mean it. I'm genuinely considering just — not going."

She smiled. "Your flight is in two hours."

"I'm aware of my flight."

She reached across the table and put her hand over his. His fingers closed around hers immediately.

"This isn't the end," she said. "This is just the middle."

He looked at her for a long moment. Outside, a motorbike went by. The fan overhead made its slow revolution. The coffee was good — she would remember that, the specific goodness of that last coffee at the Lee Café, the particular quality of morning light in that room.

"Yeah," he said. "The middle."

He repeated it the way people repeat things they are trying to make true by saying them.

She didn't mind. She believed it herself. She had believed in the middle of this story since the beginning, through every poor connection and missed call and good morning message sent into the dark. She had believed in it as an act of faith, long before she had any evidence for it.

She had evidence now.

VI.  After

She cried when he left. Not in front of him — she was not that kind of person, and she had her own particular dignity — but after, in a corner of the airport, near a pillar, standing very still with her hand pressed flat against her sternum, crying quietly the way people cry when they are not willing to make a scene but they have no other option.

She stayed that way for a few minutes. Then she stopped. She wiped her face. She found her gate.

On the plane back to Hong Kong, she looked at the photographs she had taken. The garden. The light. His face squinting into the Thai sun, laughing at something she'd said. Their hands together against the lotus pond.

She thought about what he had said, on the path between the palms.

I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be.

She put her phone against her chest — the same gesture she used when something moved her, the way her mother used to, a hand pressed to the sternum as if holding the feeling in — and she closed her eyes as the plane rose up and the Gulf fell away beneath the clouds.

She was going back to Hong Kong. He was going back to Maine. The time zones would resume. The screens would return.

But the garden was real. The café was real. His laugh, the full version, the one that occupies a room — that was real too, and it was hers now, and no screen could take it back.

She pressed her phone a little harder against her chest and felt, underneath everything else — underneath the missing and the distance and the long road still ahead of them — the specific, irreducible warmth of someone who has been, for eight days, exactly where they were supposed to be.

One year later, Richard wrote:

"One year ago today was the first time we were together."

She read it in the morning, the way she read everything from him — before coffee, before the window, before any other claim the day made on her.

She did not respond right away. She just held it.

Some things you don't answer. You just let them be true.
