The Long Way Around the World

Schoolgirl Plaid

A narrative photo essay about distance, desire, and the courage it takes to love someone across an ocean of time zones and silence.

Digital, 2025 · Hong Kong / Manila

The notification arrived at an ordinary hour, in the middle of an ordinary afternoon in Hong Kong, while I was folding laundry in a room that belonged to someone else. I typed a simple "Thank you. Nice to meet u here in Facebook." without thinking much about it.

I would wake in the dark of my small room, the city outside doing its restless nightshift work, and the first thing I'd do — before the kettle, before the window — was check my phone. I was waiting. His nights arrived into my mornings.

Two years of early mornings. Two years of messages that arrived while the city still slept.

The city outside changed its weather. Inside, I stayed steady.

"Love, I can't wait and I'm dying to see you." I had sent it quietly, without ceremony — and stood behind that open door for the rest of the week, aware of the draft it let in.

What arrived in that Manila hotel room was the weight of two years, made suddenly ordinary.

The city was doing its Manila things outside the window. But inside there was the simple and enormous fact of two people who had spoken every day for nearly two years, finally in the same air.

After all that time in two-dimensional frames, he was simply — there.

We moved carefully, the way you move around something you have wanted for a long time.

The ring came on an ordinary afternoon. My hand, slightly changed.

The world sealed itself shut. What had always been the obstacle became officially, bureaucratically impossible. We were in different countries. We were, in the truest sense, unreachable.

Months later, I would still reach for my phone to tell him something small.

The quiet after connection has its own particular weight.

I kept the ring. I kept the silences too.

Some things get lost between time zones. Some things don't.

There were hours when I put the phone face-down and looked at the ceiling and thought about what it meant to love someone you had never touched.

I looked at the message for a long time.

And then — slowly, the way you put a foot into water you're not sure of yet — I began to type.

My smile arrived unexpectedly in the middle of an ordinary morning — the moment I decided to reach back across the dark water, across all the clocks in the world.

I changed my nickname again. Off the Grid. But this time it was different — not hiding, not hurting. Just quiet, finally, the kind of quiet that follows a long story finding its shape.
